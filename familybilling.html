UX Design Case Study

Feature improvement for dental practice management SaaS, I was the UX designer on a cross-functional team.

The Problem

Each patient has a separate ledger, users spend too much time and energy billing families using the software.

The Solution

Tab-based options on a patient’s ledger allowing them to view and print individual, or combined balances for guarantors along with a new group ledger printout option.

The User

Someone who works in the front office for a dentist, orthodontist, or oral surgeon. Typically the office manager, or some type of patient-facing front desk worker.

The Goal

To reduce friction for users when viewing and printing connected balances and to create an easy way to print an entire family bill.

The Process

Empathize / Research

The business prioritized ‘family billing’ because of customer requests on UserVoice, our customer feedback portal. This project was originally assigned to another designer at the company who conducted the initial research. This involved 6 customer interviews to uncover the general pain points and competitive analysis of 4 dental practice management software solutions for this same issue.

“It's time-consuming and expensive to print out all these bills and add up the totals, but more importantly, it makes our practice look unprofessional and a little foolish. Patients have commented about our waste of paper.” - Sarah B. (user)

This is a screenshot of the original ledger design. It only allowed for invoices to be printed one at a time:

Our users were spending a lot of unnecessary time when billing a family because they had to print out individual bills for each patient and then add that total up. There was also a theme of friction when managing patient connections.

Define

The customer interviews and competitive research informed our product line manager enough to create some brief requirements for this epic. The output was this user story map that served as a roadmap for developing these features.

Once the original designer got to the ideation phase but she could not get buy-in for her solution because it involved too much change. I was asked by the UX manager to come in and pair with her to help make progress, this involved us taking a step back. After I was up-to-speed, and got everyone on the samer page, the original designer was let go and this project was reassigned to me.

Ideation

The problem was clear, but the solution was not. Many other dental practice management systems are account based, this means that there is an account that you add patients to, and the designated guarantor of that group pays for everyone in that group. Our system is not like this, it's patient based. This means that each patient must define who their guarantor is and no real “group” has been established, just a spider web of connections.

Since these relationships were already created by our users for their patients, and it can be quite complex. After multiple ideation sessions with one of our software architects, product owner, and SME, we thought the best move was to redesign the ledger to have tabs that will show a patient’s individual ledger, a combined ledger for the patients that this person is the guarantor for, and/or a combined ledger for the patient’s that are guaranteed by this patient. That’s right, a patient who is not self-responsible can be the guarantor of other patients. I sketched this up to share with my team so that we could talk it through.

We all agreed that we should move forward in this direction. Using Figma, I created mockups of every variation that could exist in the system by creating a complex family example.

A patient in the system could have a guarantor, but also be a guarantor. This is a person who is not financially self-responsible, but they are responsible for another person. This is an edge case, but we wanted to keep this existing functionality for our users, this led us to a potential 3 tab system:

  • (Patient Name) Shows the patient’s individual ledger.
  • (Guarantor For) Shows this patient’s combined guarantor ledger.
  • (Guaranteed By) Shows the combined ledger for this patient’s guarantor.

This solution involves allowing the user to view either a patient’s individual ledger, or a guarantor’s ledger, which will include all of the patients that they are financially responsible for. Statements and account history can then be printed as an individual or guarantor. We wanted to give the user the option to print guarantor statements in 2 ways, either as a combined statement, or as individual statements. Prototyping

I built an interactive prototype using Figma that allowed users to view guarantor and individual ledgers and print their statements.

In the process I also gave the ledger a bit of a facelift by wrapping the sections in cards.

The new guarantor ledger design shows combined balance totals as well as individual totals. The design then allows the user to select printing options for each. For example, when printing a combined ledger, the user can select to print a group statement, or individual statements for all of the associated patients.

Testing

I led usability testing sessions with 4 users.

What we wanted to learn:

  • Do users understand the concept of viewing a ledger as patient and as guarantor?
  • Can users complete this new flow? How long will it take them?
  • Do group ledgers have the information and layout expected?
  • Do users expect to see individual ledger views on the group ledger? Not just patient overviews. Where?
  • What do users think of the redesign of the ledger?

Key Takeaways From User Feedback:

  • 100% understand the idea of patient vs. guarantor ledger view.
  • 100% are able to complete the task of printing a guarantor statement with the average time of 27 seconds.
  • 100% prefer the updated ledger design.
  • 100% expect to be able to click on a patient's overview in a guarantor's ledger and see their individual ledger.

Additional Requests From Users:

  • Users want to be able to schedule a family together.
  • Users want to be able to assign multiple guarantors.

These requests are validated by previous customer feedback, as a result they have been added to the roadmap.

Conclusion

I presented the prototype and research findings to the PLMs along with my team and we decided to move forward with this guarantor billing solution to solve the family billing problem. Because the guarantor connections already exist in our user’s systems, we can deliver value quickly this way. Another team investigated the ledger inaccuracies and introduced several changes that remedied the issue. I worked with my development team to deliver this code, and it has been in use by our customers for a few months. We have recieved rave reviews on how flexible and time-saving this solution is.

On average this new family billing process saves our user 32 clicks, 9 keystrokes, and 1 minute and 52 seconds per family. Collectively, this new feature will save our customers millions in labor costs over the coming years.
